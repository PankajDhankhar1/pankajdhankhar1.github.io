About
I Didn't Start in Strategy — I Earned My Way There

I started my career doing what most HR folks do — screening resumes, coordinating interviews, filling positions. Nothing glamorous. But somewhere around year three, I got frustrated. We were closing roles but nobody could tell me if we were closing them well. How fast? At what cost? Were people staying? Nobody tracked it, and honestly, nobody cared to.

That bothered me. So I started measuring things myself — built trackers, ran numbers, put together reports that nobody asked for. Eventually, leadership started paying attention. That's how I moved from execution to strategy — not through a title change, but because I kept asking questions that the existing reports couldn't answer.

Today I work across talent acquisition, HR business partnership, managed services, and operations. I've managed multi-crore budgets, cut vendor costs by 20%, automated most of the grunt work out of HR, and helped teams use data instead of gut feeling. I still believe the best HR leaders are the ones who can zoom into a broken process on Monday and present a workforce plan to the board on Friday.

Case Studies
Three Problems I Walked Into — And What Happened Next
HIRING TRANSFORMATION
We Were Losing Candidates Faster Than We Could Find Them
Click to read the full story →

The situation: The company was growing fast but hiring was a bottleneck. Average time-to-hire was sitting at 58 days. Offer acceptance had dropped to 71%. Diversity numbers were flat. Every delayed hire was a delayed project — and everyone knew it but no one had fixed the process.

What I did first: I pulled apart the entire hiring funnel. Mapped every stage, timed every handoff, talked to hiring managers about where things got stuck. The problems were obvious once you looked at the data — slow feedback loops, no structured interviews, sourcing channels that hadn't been updated in years.

The changes: Rebuilt the sourcing strategy using LinkedIn Recruiter and Naukri RMS with better Boolean strings. Introduced structured interviews so we were comparing apples to apples. Set up a Power BI dashboard so everyone could see the pipeline in real time — no more "where are we on that role?" emails. Started weekly calibration calls with business leaders. Launched a diversity sourcing program and revamped the referral scheme.

What changed: Time-to-hire dropped by 35%. Offer acceptance went up to 92%. We filled over 120 critical roles that year. Diversity hiring improved by 28%. But the biggest shift was cultural — hiring became a shared responsibility, not just an HR problem.

VENDOR OPTIMIZATION
25 Vendors, No Scorecards, and a Budget That Didn't Add Up
Click to read the full story →

The situation: We had 25 staffing and HR service vendors. Some were great, some were coasting, and a few were charging premium rates for mediocre output. Nobody had ever sat down and compared them properly. SLAs were inconsistent. Annual spend was running 18% over budget and nobody could explain exactly why.

What I did first: Built a vendor scorecard — nothing fancy, just the basics: quality of candidates, cost, turnaround time, compliance track record. Ran a proper spend analysis. The data made it pretty clear which vendors were earning their fees and which ones weren't.

The changes: Consolidated from 25 to 12 vendors. Renegotiated contracts with performance-linked pricing — if you deliver, you earn more; if you don't, the rate reflects that. Set up quarterly business reviews with measurable KPIs. Put everything under one governance framework so there were no more side deals or informal arrangements.

What changed: Saved 12–20% on vendor costs. Overall project costs dropped by 18%. Client retention stayed above 95% because service quality actually improved when we stopped spreading work across too many partners.

HR AUTOMATION
Spreadsheets Were Running the HR Department — So I Replaced Them
Click to read the full story →

The situation: Almost everything in HR was manual. Onboarding checklists lived in shared drives. Leave tracking was on Excel. Compliance was a quarterly scramble. Employee queries took two days to get a response. The team was busy all the time but most of the work was administrative, not strategic.

What I did first: Mapped every HR process from end to end — onboarding, attendance, leave, payroll, performance reviews, exit. Identified which ones could be automated, which ones needed redesigning first, and which ones we could leave alone for now.

The changes: Implemented KEKA HR and SAP SuccessFactors for core HR, payroll, and performance management. Built employee self-service portals so people could handle their own leave requests, payslips, and policy questions. Connected the HRIS data to Power BI so leadership had real-time workforce analytics instead of monthly PDF reports.

What changed: Automated 80% of HR processes. Data accuracy hit 98%. Compliance became continuous, not reactive — 100% in every audit. Productivity went up 15%. Attrition dropped from 22% to 15% because we could finally see engagement problems before they turned into resignations.

Projects
Things I've Built (or Am Building)

HR Analytics Dashboard

A Power BI dashboard that tracks over 25 HR metrics in real time — attrition patterns, hiring pipeline speed, engagement trends, cost-per-hire, diversity ratios. It's built for leadership, not just HR, with drill-downs by business unit, location, and quarter. The goal was simple: stop making workforce decisions based on stale quarterly reports and gut feeling.

AI Resume Screening Tool

A screening layer that reads resumes, scores them against role-specific criteria, and ranks the shortlist before a recruiter even opens the file. It cut screening time by roughly 60% in testing while actually improving the quality of shortlists. Plugs into existing ATS platforms so recruiters don't need to change how they work — just what they skip.

Workforce Planning Simulator

A scenario planning tool that lets HR and finance model headcount needs against growth targets, expected attrition, and budget limits. You can run "what if" scenarios — what if attrition goes up 3%? What if we freeze hiring in Q3? — and see the cost and capacity impact immediately. Makes planning conversations much more productive.

Attrition Risk Predictor

A predictive model trained on our own HRIS data that flags employees likely to leave in the next 60–90 days. It looks at engagement scores, tenure, compensation benchmarks, manager ratings, and a few other signals. The point isn't to prevent every resignation — it's to make sure we're not blindsided by the ones that matter most.

Analytics
What the Dashboard Actually Does

Most HR dashboards I've seen are just prettier versions of the same monthly report — lots of charts, very little action. The one I built is different because it was designed around decisions, not metrics. Every view answers a specific question: Where are we losing people? Which roles are stuck in the pipeline? Are we spending too much on certain vendors?

It pulls data from the HRIS, ATS, and vendor systems into one place. Leadership can drill down by team, geography, or time period without asking HR to "pull the numbers." The update is continuous, not monthly.

The real impact isn't the dashboard itself — it's what changed after people started using it. Attrition hotspots get caught early. Hiring bottlenecks are visible before they become emergencies. Budget conversations happen with data on the table instead of estimates. HR stopped being the team that reports on the past and started being the team that helps plan for what's next.

How I Work
Diagnose. Design. Deploy. Optimize.
1

Diagnose — Understand Before You Act

I don't start with solutions. I start with questions. What does the data say? Where are the gaps? What have we tried before and why didn't it work? Most HR problems look different once you actually measure them instead of assuming you know the answer.

2

Design — Build the Plan Around the Outcome

Once the problem is clear, I design the intervention — with defined success metrics, stakeholder alignment, and a realistic timeline. I've learned the hard way that a brilliant strategy with no buy-in is just a PowerPoint that nobody opens again.

3

Deploy — Do the Work, Track the Progress

Execution is where most things fall apart. I set clear ownership, short feedback cycles, and make sure we're measuring adoption — not just whether we launched something, but whether people are actually using it and whether it's working.

4

Optimize — Keep What Works, Fix What Doesn't

Nothing is done after launch. I build in review checkpoints, look at leading indicators, and iterate. Some things scale. Some get killed. The point is to learn quickly and not let ego get in the way of adjusting course.
